\begin{abstract}
The two-point correlation function $\xi(r)$ is the primary summary statistic for the clustering analysis of galaxy surveys.  Its estimation via the Landy--Szalay estimator requires counting all pairs of objects at each separation, with computational cost scaling as $O(N^2)$ or, with tree acceleration, $O(N \cdot N_{\rm shell})$ per radial bin.  We show that the $k$-Nearest Neighbor Cumulative Distribution Functions (\knn{}-CDF), combined with a geometric ladder of diluted subsamples, provide an estimator of $\xi(r)$ that is mathematically equivalent to the Landy--Szalay estimator in the limit of fine binning, while requiring only $O(N_R \log N)$ computation.  We develop the full formalism including survey-geometry random catalogs and per-object weights (FKP, completeness, systematic), and demonstrate exact equivalence at the two-point level.  We analyze the error budget---showing where the \knn{} approach matches, exceeds, or falls short of brute-force pair counting---and describe the dilution ladder that extends the measurement to scales far beyond the mean inter-particle separation with built-in variance estimation from overlapping rungs.  The method requires no choice of radial binning, returns $\xi(r)$ as a continuous function, and provides all scales simultaneously from a single tree construction per dilution level.
\end{abstract}
